IFAB LAWS OF THE GAME — LAW 12: FOULS AND MISCONDUCT
Official Reference Text (2024/25 Edition)
======================================================

PART 1 — DIRECT FREE KICKS
----------------------------
A direct free kick is awarded if a player commits any of the following offences against
an opponent in a manner considered by the referee to be careless, reckless, or using
excessive force:

• Charges an opponent
• Jumps at an opponent
• Kicks or attempts to kick an opponent
• Pushes an opponent
• Strikes or attempts to strike an opponent (including head-butt)
• Tackles or challenges an opponent
• Trips or attempts to trip an opponent

CARELESS means the player shows a lack of attention or consideration when making a
challenge, with no need for a disciplinary sanction.

RECKLESS means the player acts with disregard for the danger to, or consequences for,
an opponent — a caution (yellow card) is required.

USING EXCESSIVE FORCE means the player far exceeds the necessary use of force and/or
endangers the safety of an opponent — a send-off (red card) is required.

Additional direct free-kick offences (regardless of careless/reckless/excessive force):
• Handles the ball deliberately (except goalkeeper in own penalty area)
• Commits an offence against an opponent inside the player's own penalty area
• Bites or spits at anyone
• Throws an object at the ball, an opponent, or a match official

HANDBALL RULE (Law 12.1):
A direct free kick is awarded if a player (other than the goalkeeper in their own penalty
area) deliberately handles the ball. For the purposes of determining handball offences,
the upper boundary of the arm is in line with the bottom of the armpit.

It is an offence if a player:
• Deliberately touches the ball with their hand/arm, including moving the hand/arm
  towards the ball
• Scores in the opponents' goal directly from their hand/arm (even if accidental)
• Scores in the opponents' goal immediately after the ball has touched their
  hand/arm (even if accidental)
• Creates a goal-scoring opportunity after the ball has touched their hand/arm
  (even if accidental)
• Gains possession/control of the ball with their hand/arm and then scores, or
  creates a goal-scoring opportunity

It is NOT automatically an offence if the ball touches a player's hand/arm when:
• It directly touches the player's own head, body, or foot
• The player falls and the hand/arm is between the body and the ground to support
  the body (not extended to make the body bigger)
• The hand/arm is close to the body and did not make the body unnaturally bigger


PART 2 — INDIRECT FREE KICKS
-------------------------------
An indirect free kick is awarded if:

• A goalkeeper inside their own penalty area handles a back-pass played deliberately
  with the feet by a teammate
• A goalkeeper inside their own penalty area handles the ball after it has been
  directly received from a throw-in taken by a teammate
• A goalkeeper delays getting the ball into play or wastes time
• A player plays in a dangerous manner
• A player impedes the progress of an opponent without any contact
• Any other offence not previously mentioned where play is stopped to caution or
  send off a player

DANGEROUS PLAY: Any action which, while attempting to play the ball, threatens injury
to any player (including the player themselves), such as attempting to kick the ball
when held by the goalkeeper, or going for a ball with the studs or sole of the boot at
the level of an opponent's face.


PART 3 — DISCIPLINARY ACTION
-------------------------------
A player is cautioned (YELLOW CARD) if they commit any of the following:
• Unsporting behaviour
• Dissent by word or action
• Persistent offences
• Delaying the restart of play
• Failing to respect the required distance
• Entering/re-entering or deliberately leaving the field without the referee's permission
• Simulation (attempting to deceive the referee by feigning injury or pretending to
  have been fouled — known as "diving")

A player is sent off (RED CARD) if they commit any of the following (SERIOUS FOUL PLAY):
• Serious foul play: Tackles or challenges an opponent with excessive force that
  endangers their physical integrity
• Violent conduct
• Spitting at a person
• Biting a person
• Denying an obvious goal-scoring opportunity (DOGSO):
  — To an opponent moving towards the offender's goal by an offence punishable by a
    free kick or penalty kick
• Using offensive, insulting, or abusive language and/or gestures
• Receiving a second caution in the match


PART 4 — PENALTY KICK OFFENCES
---------------------------------
A penalty kick is awarded when any of the above direct free-kick offences are committed
by a player inside their own penalty area, regardless of the position of the ball,
provided it is in play.


PART 5 — ADVANTAGE AND ADVANTAGE CLAUSE
------------------------------------------
The referee may allow play to continue if the non-offending team will benefit from the
advantage and penalise the original offence if the anticipated advantage does not
materialise at that time.


PART 6 — VAR (VIDEO ASSISTANT REFEREE) PROTOCOL
-------------------------------------------------
VAR reviews are only permitted for:
• Goal / no goal
• Penalty kick / no penalty kick
• Direct red card (not second yellow)
• Mistaken identity

The VAR checks the following criteria:
• Was there contact? (tackle, charge, push, handball)
• Was the contact careless, reckless, or with excessive force?
• Did the incident occur inside or outside the penalty area?
• Was the ball in play at the time of the offence?
• Was the player's positioning legal?

The reviewing VAR official checks video angles and advises the on-field referee, who
makes the final decision. The standard for overriding an on-field decision is "clear
and obvious error."

KEY DEFINITIONS FOR VAR REVIEW:
• TACKLE: An attempt to win the ball from an opponent using the foot or leg.
• CHARGE: Using the shoulder to jostle or push an opponent.
• OBSTRUCTION/IMPEDING: Blocking an opponent's path without playing the ball.
• SIMULATION: Deliberately falling or acting as if fouled without contact,
  intended to deceive the referee.
• LAST DEFENDER: The last outfield player between the attacker and the goal.


PART 7 — PRACTICAL GUIDANCE FOR VISUAL ANALYSIS
-------------------------------------------------
When reviewing frames from a video clip, the following visual cues are key:

1. POINT OF CONTACT: Where on the opponent's body did contact occur?
   — Leg-to-leg: possible trip or tackle
   — Arm-to-back/chest: possible push or charge
   — Foot-to-ankle: possible dangerous tackle
   — No contact: possible simulation

2. TIMING: Did the player play the ball before, simultaneously with, or after
   making contact with the opponent?
   — Ball first, then player: generally a fair challenge
   — Player first, then ball: likely a foul

3. INTENT AND BODY POSTURE: Was the player in a normal defensive position or
   were they lunging/extending in a manner that endangered the opponent?

4. FOLLOW-THROUGH: Did the player follow through with force after making contact?

5. REACTION OF FOULED PLAYER: Does the reaction appear commensurate with the
   force of contact, or is it exaggerated (simulation)?

Law 8: The Start and Restart of Play
Law 8 governs kick-offs and the dropped ball procedure. The dropped ball mechanic has been significantly fundamentally restructured to prevent the chaotic, injury-prone artificial contests of the past. The contemporary procedure is highly systematic. If play is stopped while the ball is inside the penalty area, or if the ball's last touch occurred within the penalty area, the ball is dropped exclusively for the defending team's goalkeeper.   

If play is stopped while the ball is outside the penalty area, the referee drops the ball for one player of the team that possessed the ball, or would have clearly gained possession, at the exact position where play was stopped. To ensure unimpeded restarts, all other players from both teams must remain a minimum of 4 meters (4.5 yards) away from the ball until it makes contact with the ground. The ball officially enters play the moment it touches the ground; if it is played before grounding, the drop is retaken. A goal cannot be scored directly from a dropped ball; it must touch at least two separate players before entering the net.   

Law 9: The Ball in and out of Play

Law 11: Offside
Law 11 governs the offside rule, a fundamental tactical constraint designed to prevent "goal hanging" and encourage cohesive team movement. The rule is bifurcated into two elements: being in an offside position, and committing an offside offence.   

A player is categorized as being in an offside position if any part of their head, body, or feet is situated in the opponents' half of the field and is nearer to the opponents' goal line than both the ball and the second-last opponent. For the purposes of this geometric determination, the hands and arms of all players, including goalkeepers, are strictly ignored. The upper boundary of the arm is legally defined as being in line with the bottom of the armpit.   

Merely occupying an offside position is not an offence. An infraction only crystallizes if the player becomes involved in active play by interfering with the play (touching the ball), interfering with an opponent (clearly obstructing their line of vision or challenging for the ball), or gaining an advantage. Gaining an advantage is defined as playing a ball that has rebounded off a goalpost, crossbar, match official, or opponent, or playing a ball that has been deliberately saved. A "save" is specifically defined as an attempt to stop a ball going into or very close to the goal with any part of the body other than the hands/arms (unless it is the goalkeeper within their penalty area).   

If an attacker in an offside position receives the ball from an opponent who deliberately played the ball, no offside offence has occurred. Deliberate play necessitates the player having control of the ball with the genuine possibility of passing, gaining possession, or clearing it, evaluated by criteria such as the ball traveling from a distance with a clear view and moving at a manageable speed.   

To ensure technological and observational consistency, the point of contact used to evaluate offside positioning is the first point of contact when the ball is played by a teammate. However, a newly codified exception exists: when a goalkeeper throws the ball, the last point of contact upon the release of the throw is utilized as the exact reference point, providing a much clearer binary frame for match officials and Video Assistant Referees assessing rapid counter-attacks.

Exhaustive Specification of Law 12: Fouls and Misconduct
Law 12 constitutes the primary judicial instrument for maintaining physical safety, tactical fairness, and sporting integrity. It categorically defines the rigid criteria for physical fouls, technical infractions, and behavioral misconduct, subsequently outlining the precise mechanisms for game restarts and the escalation of disciplinary sanctions. Direct Free Kick Infractions
A direct free kick is awarded to the opposing team if a player commits any of a specific set of physical offences against an opponent in a manner evaluated by the referee to fall into one of three distinct thresholds of severity: careless, reckless, or using excessive force.   

The physical actions that trigger this evaluation include:

Charging an opponent.   

Jumping at an opponent.   

Kicking or attempting to kick an opponent.   

Pushing an opponent.   

Striking or attempting to strike (including the execution of a head-butt) an opponent.   

Tackling or challenging an opponent.   

Tripping or attempting to trip an opponent.   

The legal definitions of the severity thresholds dictate the corresponding disciplinary sanction. Careless implies the player exhibited a lack of attention or consideration when initiating a challenge; this requires a direct free kick but necessitates no further disciplinary sanction. Reckless indicates the player acted with complete disregard for the danger to, or consequences for, their opponent; this mandates both a direct free kick and a caution (yellow card). Using excessive force means the player exceeded the necessary use of force, thereby actively endangering the safety of an opponent; this mandates a direct free kick and a sending-off (red card).   

Beyond the severity-evaluated challenges, a direct free kick is definitively awarded for the following absolute infractions, regardless of the force applied:

Committing a handball offence (explicitly excluding the goalkeeper while operating within their own defined penalty area).   

Holding an opponent, which restricts their physical movement.   

Impeding the progress of an opponent coupled with physical contact.   

Biting or spitting at any individual listed on the team rosters or a match official.   

Throwing an object at the match ball, an opponent, or a match official, or making unnatural contact with the ball utilizing a held object.   

If any of these direct free kick offences are committed by a defending player within the spatial confines of their own penalty area, the sanction is elevated, and a penalty kick is awarded to the opposing team. A critical tactical provision states that if a defender begins holding an attacker outside the penalty area and continues that hold into the penalty area, the referee is legally mandated to award a penalty kick.   

Indirect Free Kick Infractions
Indirect free kicks are reserved for offences that are predominantly technical, involve no direct physical contact with the opponent, or constitute highly specific infractions committed by goalkeepers operating within their own penalty area.

An indirect free kick is awarded to the opposition if a field player:

Plays in a dangerous manner, such as raising a boot near an opponent's head. However, executing a scissors or bicycle kick is expressly permissible provided it is performed in a manner that is not dangerous to an opponent.   

Impedes the progress of an opponent without any physical contact. This is defined as moving deliberately into an opponent's path to obstruct, block, or slow them down when the ball is not within playing distance of either individual. All players retain a right to their position on the field; merely being in the way is legal, whereas moving into the way constitutes an infraction.   

Actively prevents the goalkeeper from releasing the ball from their hands, or kicks/attempts to kick the ball while the goalkeeper is in the physical process of releasing it.   

Initiates a deliberate, choreographed trick to pass the ball to the goalkeeper utilizing the head, chest, knee, etc., designed specifically to circumvent the back-pass law. This offence is penalized whether or not the goalkeeper actually touches the ball with their hands; the initiator of the trick is the penalized party.   

Commits any other unlisted offence for which the referee halts play strictly to caution or send off a player.   

The Goalkeeper Paradigm Shift: The Eight-Second Rule
Historically, goalkeepers were restricted to controlling the ball with their hands for a maximum of six seconds, an infraction penalized by an indirect free kick. The 2025/2026 regulations have fundamentally restructured this protocol to eliminate subjective enforcement and aggressively deter tactical time-wasting delays.   

A goalkeeper is legally considered to be in control of the ball when it is secured between their hands, trapped between a hand and any surface (including the ground or their own body), touching any part of the hands or arms (excluding accidental rebounds during a save), held in an outstretched open hand, or while being bounced or thrown in the air. Once this control is established, the goalkeeper cannot be lawfully challenged by an opponent.   

Under the revolutionary updated framework, a goalkeeper who controls the ball with their hand(s) or arm(s) for a duration exceeding eight seconds will be penalized. Crucially, the restart for this temporal offence is no longer an indirect free kick. Instead, a corner kick is awarded to the opposing team. This corner kick is to be taken from the corner area nearest to the goalkeeper's exact position at the moment they were penalized.   

To ensure absolute transparency and provide a clear warning, the referee is now mandated to visually count down the final five seconds of this eight-second allowance by visibly raising their hand. The rules explicitly state that no disciplinary action (yellow card) is taken for exceeding the eight seconds unless the goalkeeper commits the offence repeatedly in a persistent manner.   

An indirect free kick remains the standard sanction for a goalkeeper who, while inside their penalty area:

Touches the ball with the hand/arm after releasing it and before it has subsequently touched another player.   

Touches the ball with the hand/arm after it has been deliberately kicked to them by a teammate (the standard back-pass rule).   

Touches the ball with the hand/arm immediately after receiving it directly from a throw-in executed by a teammate.

Video Assistant Referee (VAR) Protocol and Technological Integration
The utilization of Video Match Officials (VMOs) under Law 6 has fundamentally altered the epistemology of refereeing, ensuring that "clear and obvious errors" and "serious missed incidents" are rectified with high fidelity.   

Core Principles of the VAR System
The VAR operates independently from a dedicated Video Operation Room (VOR), assisted by Assistant VARs (AVARs) and Replay Operators (ROs). The VAR system continuously and automatically checks footage for four specific categories of match-changing incidents: Goal/No Goal, Penalty/No Penalty, Direct Red Cards (excluding second yellow cards historically), and Mistaken Identity. Because all critical incidents are automatically checked, players and coaches are not required, nor permitted, to request reviews.   

A fundamental principle dictates that the on-field referee must always make an initial decision; they are strictly prohibited from issuing "no decision" and defaulting to the VAR. The referee's original decision stands unless the video review clearly demonstrates a clear and obvious error. While the VAR can recommend a review, only the referee possesses the authority to initiate one, and the final decision always rests with the referee. Accuracy is prioritized over speed; therefore, there is no maximum time limit imposed on the review process.   

Check vs. Review Procedures
During an automatic check, if no error is found (a "silent check"), the VAR typically does not communicate with the referee, though they may provide confirmation to aid in match management. If the referee needs to delay a restart to allow the check to complete, they signal this by holding a finger to their earpiece and extending their other arm.   

A formal review is initiated when the VAR recommends it or the referee suspects a serious incident was missed. The referee stops play (ideally in a neutral zone) and executes the "TV signal" (a rectangular gesture).   

On-Field Review (OFR): Utilized for subjective decisions, such as assessing the intensity of a foul or determining offside interference. The referee views the footage at a dedicated Referee Review Area (RRA). Normal speed replays are used to evaluate intensity, while slow-motion is reserved for identifying exact points of contact.   

VAR-Only Review: Utilized for purely factual, geographical decisions, such as whether a foul occurred inside or outside the penalty area, the exact point of contact for a handball, or determining if the ball crossed the boundary line.   

Following the review, the referee displays the TV signal again and communicates the final decision, proceeding to take, change, or rescind disciplinary actions. Competitions now possess the option to allow referees to publicly announce and explicitly explain their final decisions to the stadium audience and television viewers via the public address system, enhancing transparency.   

Strict behavioral codes surround the technology. Any player, substitute, or team official who enters the RRA or excessively displays the TV signal will be cautioned. Any personnel entering the VOR will be immediately sent off. A match's validity is legally insulated; it is not invalidated due to VAR technology malfunctions, incorrect VAR-related decisions, or decisions not to review an incident. If a VAR official becomes incapacitated, they must be replaced by a qualified individual; if no replacement is available, the match continues without VAR.   

Expansions to the VAR Protocol (2025-2027)
To maximize the utility of the VAR system without compromising the flow of the game, the IFAB AGM approved three significant adjustments to the protocol, expanding the scope of reviewable incidents based on the presence of clear evidence :   

Incorrect Second Yellow Cards: The VAR is now permitted to assist the referee in relation to red cards that arise from a clearly incorrect second yellow card. Previously, VAR intervention was strictly limited to direct, straight red card offences.   

Expanded Mistaken Identity: The VAR can now intervene when the referee penalizes the wrong team entirely for an offence, resulting in a red or yellow card being shown to the incorrect player.   

Corner Kick Rectification: Competitions have the option to allow VARs to review clearly incorrectly awarded corner kicks. However, this is heavily caveated: the review is only permitted if it can be completed immediately and without causing any delay to the restart of play.   

These VAR expansions occur alongside ongoing trials for Semi-Automated Offside Technology (SAOT) and Football Video Support (FVS), which seek to automate factual decision-making and further reduce the temporal footprint of video reviews.   

Second and Third-Order Tactical Implications
The integration of these updated laws generates profound second and third-order effects on the tactical execution and psychological landscape of the sport. The regulatory shifts dictate spatial, temporal, and strategic adaptations for coaching staffs globally.

The transition from a six-second indirect free kick rule to an eight-second corner kick rule for goalkeepers fundamentally alters high-pressing triggers. The previous rule was notoriously subjective. By mandating a visible countdown for the final five seconds, referees enforce the rule overtly. The punitive sanction of a corner kick is statistically devastating compared to an indirect free kick. Consequently, opposing forwards will utilize the referee's visual countdown as an absolute trigger to initiate high-intensity presses, knowing the goalkeeper is under acute temporal duress and may be forced into an erratic clearance to avoid conceding a corner.   

The mathematical disadvantage created by the one-minute waiting period for slow substitutions and off-field injury assessments acts as a severe deterrent. A team attempting to protect a narrow lead by feigning cramps will now face a full minute of active play with only ten men. The statistical risk of conceding a goal while numerically disadvantaged mathematically outweighs the few seconds gained by the initial delay. This inherently compels rapid transitions and radically increases the proportion of effective playing time.   

Furthermore, the allowance for VAR to silently review incorrectly awarded corner kicks closes a significant loophole in set-piece capitalization. Historically, an incorrectly awarded corner resulting in a goal was an unreviewable sequence. By permitting immediate checks on the out-of-play touch, the defensive integrity of the match is preserved. Additionally, utilizing the last point of contact for offside determinations during a goalkeeper throw eliminates the ambiguity of the throwing motion. A throw is a continuous kinetic action; measuring offside at the exact millisecond of release provides video operators with a binary frame, drastically accelerating VAR offside checks on rapid counter-attacks.   

Conclusion
The IFAB Laws of the Game for the current 2025-2027 cycle represent a highly sophisticated, rigorously calibrated regulatory system. Through an exhaustive specification of Law 12, it is evident that the threshold for fouls, technical misconduct, and goalkeeper possession is tightly defined to protect both player welfare and the integrity of the match. The transformation of the goalkeeper possession rule into an eight-second limit, heavily penalized by a corner kick, stands as a monumental shift in spatial punishment.   

Coupled with the "Only the captain" guidelines, the ten-second substitution exit mandate, the one-minute off-field injury wait, and the continual expansion of the Video Assistant Referee protocol to cover second yellow cards and corner kicks, the modern rulebook operates as an aggressive mechanism designed to enforce continuous play. This legal framework leaves minimal ambiguity, utilizing strict chronological metrics and visual countdowns to eliminate subjectivity in time-wasting, thereby ensuring that the outcome of a match is determined exclusively by the technical, tactical, and athletic merits of the players while the ball is in active play.
